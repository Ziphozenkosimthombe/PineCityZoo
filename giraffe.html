GIRAFFA

The Giraffe (Giraffa camelopardalis) is the world’s tallest land mammal, belonging to the family Giraffidae. With their incredibly long necks, distinctive spotted coats, and towering legs, giraffes are one of Africa’s most iconic animals. There are four recognized species, including the Masai giraffe, Reticulated giraffe, Northern giraffe, and Southern giraffe, each with unique coat patterns.

A giraffe’s neck alone can measure up to 2.4 meters (8 feet) and contains only seven vertebrae—the same number as humans! Their long, prehensile tongues (up to 45 cm / 18 inches) help them pluck leaves from thorny acacia trees, their favorite food. Despite their height, giraffes only need 5–30 minutes of sleep per day, often standing up to avoid predators.

Giraffes are social but loosely structured, living in fluid herds that change frequently. Males, called bulls, engage in "necking" battles to establish dominance, swinging their heavy heads and ossicone-covered skulls like clubs. Females, or cows, are highly protective of their calves, which can stand within 30 minutes of birth.
