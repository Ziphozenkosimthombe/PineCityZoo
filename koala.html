KOALA

The Koala (Phascolarctos cinereus) is one of Australia’s most beloved marsupials, known for its fluffy ears, spoon-shaped nose, and sleepy demeanor. Despite often being called a "koala bear," this adorable creature is not a bear at all—it’s a marsupial, related to kangaroos and wombats. Koalas are perfectly adapted to life in eucalyptus forests, where they spend up to 20 hours a day resting and 2-4 hours feeding.

Koalas have a highly specialized diet, eating only certain types of eucalyptus leaves, which are toxic to most animals. Their sharp claws and strong limbs help them climb trees with ease, while their thick fur protects them from harsh weather. An adult koala can weigh 4-15 kg (9-33 lbs), with males being larger than females. One of their most distinctive features is their loud, deep bellow, used by males to communicate territory.

These solitary animals are mostly active at night and live in home ranges that overlap with other koalas. Sadly, koalas are facing habitat loss, bushfires, and disease, leading to their classification as an endangered species in some regions.
